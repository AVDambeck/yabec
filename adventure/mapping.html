Mapping

Dungeons are complex environments where it is easy to get lost. Keeping a map is essential. To keep the dungeon mysterious and labrynthine, it's essential to maintain the fog of war by having the dungeon master not provide an exact map.

The Dungeon Master should avoid looking at the players map, and avoid perfecting the players map.

Its recommended that one player take on the role of Mapper. See Roles.

White Board Method

The dungeon master has a clip board, a page protector, 1\2 or 1\4 graph paper, and a dry erase marker. They can draw out the current and near rooms. Its upto the players to copy and connect the rooms. As the players adventure deeper into the dungeon, the dungeon master erases and draws new areas.

Flow Chart Method

Rather than track the exact dimensions of every room, the mapper might opt to keep a general shape of the dungeon with a loose flow chart, labeling rooms and their connections.

Calls

While not in an encounter, the mapper asks the dungeon master for a call. Each call should have an unambiguous meaning. The mapper and Dungeon Master should work together to establish calls that work for them.

GFC's video is a perfect explanation of dungeon mapping. This page is a written guide based on their advice.

Additionally, you may wish to modify the system to better suit your needs. Any non-ambiguous agreed upon system will work.

Restrictions

The Dungeon Master will only provide calls when the players ask, and only after they enter a room and are able to spend time observing it.
